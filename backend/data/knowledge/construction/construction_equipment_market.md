# Construction Equipment Market Dynamics

## Infrastructure Spending as the Primary Demand Driver

Construction equipment demand is tied more directly to government infrastructure spending and private construction starts than to commodity cycles. Key macroeconomic indicators to monitor:
- **Public works budgets**: US Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA, 2021, USD 1.2 trillion over 10 years) is the largest sustained driver of North American construction equipment demand in a generation. Highways, bridges, rail, ports, water infrastructure.
- **Housing starts**: Residential construction is the highest-volume segment for compact equipment (mini-excavators, skid steers, compact track loaders). Interest rate sensitivity is high.
- **Industrial construction**: Data centres, semiconductor fabs, EV battery plants, and LNG terminals have driven large-scale earthmoving and crane demand post-2022.
- **Energy transition infrastructure**: Solar and wind farm construction requires site preparation, access road construction, and cable-trench excavation — growing addressable market for mid-sized excavators (20–50 tonne class).

## Rental vs Purchase Dynamics

The construction market has shifted significantly toward rental over the past two decades. In North America, approximately 55–60% of construction equipment is rented rather than owned by contractors. Key rental aggregators — United Rentals, Sunbelt, BlueLine — have become major OEM customers, often purchasing fleet in bulk at discounted rates and making brand decisions based on resale value, parts availability, and telematics integration.

Implications for OEM strategy:
- Rental companies prioritise total cost of ownership and fleet standardisation
- Resale value (residual value) is a critical competitive factor; OEMs with stronger brand recognition command better auction prices
- Telematics-based utilisation data from rental customers is increasingly valuable for product development

## OEM Competitive Landscape in Construction

**Caterpillar** is the global market leader in large construction equipment (motor graders, large wheel loaders, large dozers). Cat dominates the North American dealer channel and benefits from strong brand loyalty among large civil contractors.

**Komatsu** is the #2 construction OEM globally by revenue. Komatsu's D61/D65/D85 dozers and HB215LC hybrid excavator are strong competitive products. Komatsu's weakness historically has been North American construction dealer network depth vs Caterpillar.

**Volvo Construction Equipment (Volvo CE)** is the #3 player globally, with strength in articulated haulers, compact equipment, and European markets. Volvo CE's electric equipment line (EC230 Electric excavator, L120 Electric wheel loader) is the most mature in the industry.

**Liebherr** has strong positions in tower cranes and large crawler cranes (LTM, LR series), competing less directly with Komatsu in earthmoving.

**John Deere Construction** is a meaningful competitor in North America for excavators (130G–470G class) and motor graders, leveraging its agricultural dealer network.

**Hitachi Construction Machinery** competes directly with Komatsu in hydraulic excavators, especially in Japan and Southeast Asia. Hitachi-Deere JV covers North America.

## Key Signals for Construction Equipment Demand

- Infrastructure bill project award timelines (state DOT, FHWA data)
- Housing permits and starts data (US Census Bureau monthly)
- PMI construction sub-index and AGC (Associated General Contractors) surveys
- Rental fleet utilisation rates (United Rentals, Sunbelt quarterly earnings)
- Cement consumption and steel rebar imports (leading indicators for construction starts)
- Large project pipeline databases (Dodge Data, ConstructConnect)

## Compact Equipment Growth

The fastest-growing segment in construction equipment by unit volume is compact equipment: mini-excavators (<6 tonne), compact track loaders, telescopic handlers. These are dominated by Doosan Bobcat (subsidiary of Doosan), John Deere, and Case. Komatsu has a smaller position in this segment. Growth is driven by residential housing, landscaping, and urban infrastructure where large machines cannot manoeuvre. This segment is also the most price-sensitive and has the shortest replacement cycles (5–8 years vs 12–15 for large equipment).
